ABSTRACT

The Smart Barangay Kiosk System is an offline-first Progressive Web Application (PWA) developed to modernize document request handling and resident management in Philippine barangays. Built with React 19, Vite, Supabase, and TailwindCSS, the system provides a self-service kiosk interface deployed on a tablet with a Bluetooth thermal printer, an administrative dashboard for barangay officials, a resident portal for remote document requests with full offline capability, and a super-administrator panel for multi-barangay management. The system supports eleven barangay document types—Barangay Clearance, Certificate of Indigency, Certificate of Residency, Certificate of Good Moral Character, Certification for First Time Job Seeker, Certification for Monthly Income, Certification for Solo Parent, Certification PDA, Business Clearance, Barangay ID, and Barangay Certification—with a custom “Other documents” option, automatic control-number generation, receipt printing, SMS notifications via PhilSMS, and full offline functionality across both kiosk and resident portal interfaces through IndexedDB caching and Workbox service workers. Both the kiosk terminal and the resident portal allow document request submission without an internet connection; requests are queued locally and synchronized automatically when connectivity is restored. The system was pilot-tested in Barangay Maangas and Barangay Sta. Maria in Presentacion, Camarines Sur. Evaluation results indicate that the system significantly reduced document processing time, improved resident accessibility, and enhanced administrative efficiency, demonstrating the viability of low-cost, offline-first digital solutions for rural local governance.

Chapter 1
INTRODUCTION

1.1. Background of the Study

Local Government Units (LGUs) in the Philippines have been at the forefront of promoting digital transformation in public service delivery in recent years [1]. At the most fundamental level, the barangay is the smallest administrative unit responsible for implementing government functions. Most barangays still process documents, organize files, and manage resident information through manual and paper-based systems. These conventional methods, though familiar, are often inefficient and time-consuming, especially during peak seasons when the demand for barangay documents significantly increases.

Digitalization of service delivery, however, has its own disadvantages. Services are usually slow and painfully long, especially during peak seasons. Residents sometimes have to wait for hours to process a simple document request [2]. The current system is inconvenient, causing frustration for senior citizens, persons with disabilities, and those short on time due to work or other personal obligations.

A number of residents are unable to access some essential barangay services because of poor digital infrastructure and internet connectivity within their respective localities [3]. Urban areas might benefit from high-speed internet services, while poor rural barangays are unable to participate in digital programs [4]. While some municipalities already share online portals, this cannot guarantee that most residents, especially those in rural areas, own smartphones, computers, or stable internet connections [5]. Hence, these services may actually exclude them from the government’s e-Governance scheme. Instead of being inclusive, digital initiatives may further widen the gap between digitally connected and disconnected communities [6].

The effective use of low-cost and accessible technologies has enabled the development of affordable, simple, and efficient kiosks for community service delivery [7]. These systems provide promising opportunities to create digital platforms that work even in areas with limited power and network connectivity. The Smart Barangay Kiosk System allows residents to request documents and barangay officials to manage resident records without requiring residents to own a personal device or maintain an internet connection. The kiosk acts as a digital gateway for the community—easy to use, always available, and not dependent on the resident’s access to technology.

In addition to being accessible, the system simplifies administrative processes, lessens the load on staff, and encourages transparency in the community. Staff spend less time manually managing walk-in requests and can carry out actions such as verifying, approving, and updating records through the system. It keeps all transactions digitally recorded and reduces the chances of misplacing paper documents or having inconsistent data.

Thus, the present study focuses on the development of an integrated Smart Barangay Kiosk System for document request handling and resident management—a step forward in the practical direction of modernizing barangay operations in consonance with national goals of digital transformation. The use of a tablet-based kiosk with a Bluetooth thermal printer in this context emphasizes low-cost innovation that suits the needs and capabilities of local communities [7].

Municipalities have been enhancing web platforms through which residents may request documents. On the other hand, for those living on the wrong side of the digital divide—especially in rural setups or low-income barangays—the use of online portals is a big challenge, thereby posing an obstacle for citizens to access essential public services [3]. Digital transformation must consider the availability of both infrastructure and user access. In many barangays, the residents are limited by not only weak connectivity but also the issue of personal digital devices and computer literacy [5]. Many residents are unaware or unable to use online platforms, despite their availability in the area.

It becomes clear that there is an immediate need for a localized, offline-capable, and simple-to-use platform through which every resident can access barangay services, taking into account their level of technology access. The need is not just for innovation, but for inclusive innovation—one that reaches every member of the community, regardless of their digital limitations [6].

Under the circumstances, the Smart Barangay Kiosk System digitizes critical barangay functions via a touchscreen tablet kiosk stationed in the barangay hall. It enables residents to request documents from a self-reliant system without using their own devices. The kiosk is installed in a public and easily accessible location, allowing even the least tech-savvy individuals to navigate basic services.

The kiosk also features a remote-management administrative dashboard that enables authorized barangay officials to manage resident records and process document requests. This dashboard allows officials to have real-time information for managing citizen requests more effectively and maintaining timely and accurate records. Improvement in quality is not limited to the service area alone but has also been made for accountability and transparency in public administration [8].

The system operates in an offline-first manner, integrating into rural barangays with the advantage of operating effectively even under conditions with limited or no internet connectivity for extended periods. When internet access becomes available, the system automatically synchronizes data with the Supabase cloud server, keeping everything updated without needing manual uploads. This two-way mode (offline and online) increases the reliability of the system.

The combination of all these features with the use of a consumer-grade Android tablet and a portable Bluetooth thermal printer makes this solution cost-effective, scalable, and sustainable for community use. This system can be adopted and maintained by small barangays without large budgets or extensive technical staff. Its compact and energy-efficient properties make it an ideal system for remote areas with poor or unreliable power supply.

The Smart Barangay Kiosk System aims to enhance access to document services and secure resident data management, complemented by offline functionality to promote digital inclusion within the community. Through this, technology is given a role in local community efforts toward transparency, access, and delivery of services [8]. By transforming how barangays interact with their constituents, this innovation contributes not only to improved service delivery but also to building trust and participation among residents, strengthening the relationship between the government and the people it serves.

1.2. Statement of the Problem

Manual and repetitive processes continue to undermine the efficiency of barangay offices. Residents must physically go to the barangay hall to request documents. Processing time is slow and documents are often misfiled. This age-old system restricts access to documentation services, especially for the elderly or those unable to travel during regular business hours.

Moreover, there is no central digital system where resident data is stored and managed. This lack makes it difficult to track which documents have been requested and verify records. Contact between officials and residents is limited; document requests are mostly handled through in-person visits that are not efficient for all.

This continues to cause slow document processing, inefficient management of resident data, and reduced transparency, as well as making already difficult access to digital platforms worse among residents who do not have personal devices or reliable internet access. The absence of a central system for request handling, transaction tracking, or timely availability of public information causes inconsistency in service delivery while the administrative workload remains heavy.

That is why there is a call for a cost-efficient, low-power, and scalable solution that can digitize core barangay services and run even in conditions of limited connectivity. Such a solution should also improve both resident satisfaction and administrative efficiency when it comes to resolving administrative requests. Additionally, challenges related to system deployment, adoption, and maintenance must be addressed to ensure sustainability at the barangay level.

1.4. Objectives

The main objective of the study is to develop, design, and implement a Smart Barangay Kiosk System that will modernize and digitize the way barangay services are delivered to the community. With the use of cheap and affordable technology, particularly a tablet-based kiosk platform with Bluetooth thermal printing, the system will be able to improve the working efficiency of the government while making accessibility easier to all residents, especially those who have limited access to digital resources, and promote transparency within local governance.

The following are the specific objectives of the study:

  1. To develop a kiosk interface that allows residents to safely and easily request barangay documents such as Certificates of Indigency, Barangay Clearances, and other essential services with or without the need for a personal computer or smartphone.
  2. To construct a secure and centralized database for storing and managing resident profiles, service requests, and other barangay-related records.
  3. To create a web-based administrative dashboard accessible remotely by authorized barangay personnel for real-time management, tracking, and processing of requests, including adding, editing, and searching residents.
  4. To automate the printing of a receipt for the walk-in and provision of SMS notifications to alert residents of document availability for collection.
  5. To develop offline-first functionality that guarantees continuous system operation during internet outages and allows automatic data synchronization once connectivity is restored.
  6. To implement basic user authentication and security measures that help ensure data privacy and prevent unauthorized access.
  7. To test and evaluate the system’s usability, reliability, and efficiency through deployment in one or two pilot barangays.
1.5. Scope and Delimitations

This study mainly covers the design, development, and evaluation of the prototype for the Smart Barangay Kiosk System. The scope of this study is limited to the following:

  • Focuses on designing, developing, and evaluating a Smart Barangay Kiosk System using a tablet kiosk with Bluetooth thermal printer.
  • Covers core modules: Document Request (kiosk and resident portal), Resident Management, Admin Dashboard, Super-Admin Dashboard, Offline-First Operation, Auto-Print Receipts, SMS Notifications, Announcements and Events, Resident Verification, Feedback Collection, and Audit Logging.
  • Testing and implementation are limited to Barangay Maangas and Barangay Sta. Maria in Presentacion, Camarines Sur.
  • Payment is cash only upon pickup.
  • The prototype supports requests for the following barangay documents:
    • Barangay Clearance – a Philippine government document from the local barangay confirming the resident is in good moral standing with no pending issues, needed for jobs, business permits, loans, or opening bank accounts.
    • Certificate of Indigency – an official document issued by the barangay certifying that a resident is a member of an indigent or less fortunate family.
    • Certificate of Residency – an official document verifying that an individual is a legal and bona fide resident of a particular barangay.
    • Certificate of Good Moral Character – a document certifying that the resident is of good moral character as attested by the barangay.
    • Certification for First Time Job Seeker – a document from the barangay hall, issued under Republic Act 11261 (First Time Jobseekers Assistance Act), that certifies the resident is seeking first-time employment.
    • Certification for Monthly Income – a document certifying the estimated monthly income of a resident, often required for scholarship applications and government aid programs.
    • Certification for Solo Parent – an official document issued by the barangay, in coordination with the DSWD, that certifies an individual’s status as a solo parent under the law.
    • Certification PDA – a certification for Persons Deprived of Assistance, verifying a resident’s eligibility status.
    • Business Clearance – a clearance document required for business permit applications and renewals within the barangay.
    • Barangay ID – an identification card issued by the barangay to verified residents.
    • Barangay Certification – a general-purpose certification document issued by the barangay for various official purposes.
    • Other documents – a custom option allowing residents to specify a document type not listed above.

The following are the delimitations of the study:

  • No integration into any national government system nor any third-party platform.
  • No mobile app development except for the Progressive Web App (PWA) version.
  • Security is just basic, advanced cybersecurity is not being studied.
  • Evaluation is limited to 2 pilot barangays, which may not represent the entire barangay.

RELATED LITERATURE REVIEW AND STUDIES

2.1. Theoretical Basis

This section describes the theories which form the core of the design of the Smart Barangay Kiosk System: e-governance, offline-first web architectures, and the principles of human-centered design. These explain in a logical and scientific manner why the researchers were able to develop a low-cost yet highly resilient system that responds to the specifics of barangay-level issues in the Philippines such as erratic connectivity and low technical literacy among the population.

2.1.1. E-Governance

E-Governance refers to the employment of information and communication technologies (ICT) meant to improve the delivery of public services, transparency, and citizen engagement in governmental processes. According to the DILG, this further enhances the PDP 2023–2028 through accountability and efficiency in local administrations at the barangay level. Programs like the Seal of Good Local Governance for Barangays (SGLGB) and the Retooled Community Support Program (RCSP) demand that digital means be explored for lessening bureaucratic bottlenecks in document requests and resident management systems, thus contributing to the national goal of developing truly inclusive communities [9]. The Smart Barangay Kiosk System applies this through provisions made for offline document requests, complemented with SMS alerts, resulting in reduced queues and paperwork and ensuring traceability—the very reason for DILG promoting digitized barangay services in low-resource settings.

2.1.2. Offline-First Progressive Web Apps (PWAs)

Offline-first PWAs encourage online access and use service workers for local storage and caching operations (e.g., IndexedDB), so that synchronization can automatically resume once the app comes back online. The architecture is essentially one of progressive enhancement, where core functionalities are targeted for offline use, and enhancements depending on network availability are added for online use [10]. In the Philippine context, where connectivity compromises reliability for rural barangays, these are enabling the development of resilient applications. With Supabase employing local cache for offline handling of requests, actions are synchronized in real time, thus assuring complete offline work for residents’ submissions and administrative approvals during the operational phase [11].

2.1.3. HCI for Low-Literacy Users

HCI states that interfaces should seek to be intuitive and minimize cognitive load, especially for a broad range of users such as elderly residents or those with limited exposure to technology. A recent study pertaining to rural UX design in the Philippines has advocated multilanguage support (English/Filipino) and large-button touch interfaces for maximum accessibility [12]. In the context of the current work, the simple nature of the kiosk forms (name, address, purpose) with automatic receipt printing minimizes errors. The responsive design of the PWA works on tablets and phones without login hurdles for residents, thereby increasing usability of the system in barangay halls even during brownouts and long queues.

2.1.4. Database Management & BaaS

Modern DBMS platforms have the full capability of ensuring data integrity in real time while synchronizing offline. Supabase (PostgreSQL-based BaaS) provides this feature through edge functions with local caching. For Philippine capstone projects in 2023–2024, Supabase has been the appropriate choice for scalable yet affordable backends of community apps, providing authentication and SMS integrations without the burden of heavy server management. In this system, Supabase manages resident records and pending requests in auto-sync mode, thereby maintaining consistency between kiosk and admin dashboard while being compliant with the data privacy restrictions laid out in the Data Privacy Act of 2012 [13].

2.1.5. Tablet-Based Kiosk Deployment

The use of consumer-grade tablets as kiosk platforms has become an increasingly viable alternative to custom embedded hardware. Android tablets offer touchscreen interaction, Wi-Fi and Bluetooth connectivity, built-in battery backup, and the ability to run Progressive Web Apps through the Chromium browser, all at a fraction of the cost of purpose-built kiosk hardware [7]. When paired with a Bluetooth thermal printer such as the GOOJPRT PT-210, the tablet kiosk provides the same receipt-printing capability as traditional POS systems while maintaining portability and low power consumption. This setup is particularly suitable for barangay halls where space, budget, and technical expertise are limited.

2.1.6. System Security and SMS Integration

All security provisions with respect to e-Governance systems must address authentication, access control, and role-based restrictions to protect sensitive resident data. Philippine guidelines from DILG require secure APIs and hashing for barangay archives. The study employs Supabase Auth with bcrypt password hashing, rate-limited kiosk password validation, OTP-based password recovery via PhilSMS, and comprehensive audit logging for all administrative sessions. SMS notifications are sent through the PhilSMS gateway for affordable and reliable notifications in the Philippines when documents are ready for pickup [11].

2.2. Related Studies

This section considers several studies, both foreign and local, about barangay management and kiosks as innovations and their gaps. While some prior works achieved digitization of records, very few integrated full offline PWAs, physical kiosks, SMS, or multi-tenant support in real deployments—gaps addressed by this study.

2.2.1. Community Data Management Using IoT Devices (Foreign)

In 2019, Adekunle and Oladipo developed an IoT-enabled kiosk for rural communities in Africa for self-service submission of forms to be stored locally, with auto-sync. The multilingual interface emphasized maximum robustness in a low-connectivity environment to limit manual bottlenecks [16]. This validates offline kiosks for developing economies, but they did not include SMS integration or alignment with Philippine LGU procedures for document readiness alerts, which are integrated into the Smart Barangay Kiosk.

2.2.2. Design and Implementation of a Local E-Government Portal (Local)

Reyes and Salonga (2021) established an online portal for barangay clearance services with a section for tracking status and real-time notification. Although this improved access, not all areas have a constant internet connection, rendering it limited. Usability evaluation reduced foot traffic; however, the results showed that hybrid offline systems are necessary. This study builds upon it by using PWA offline caching and kiosk hardware [17].

2.2.3. Barangay Management Information System (Local)

According to Hernandez and Cruz (2021), resident records and permits were digitized and files were created to streamline administrative tasks and reduce errors through the use of SQL databases [18]. However, it lacked functionality for self-service by residents or offline modes, thus requiring in-person office visits. This system extends that by allowing no-login kiosk requests and automatic thermal printing.

2.2.4. WebYu: A Barangay Yuson Web-based Information Management System (Local)

Santos et al. in 2025 deployed WebYu in Barangay Yuson, Nueva Ecija, using Scrum methodology for automated workflows and with UX evaluated using the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) having a mean score of 4.74 out of 5. It decreased certification time to only 32% of its former duration, but it relied on online access. The dashboard concept is enhanced in this study through offline PWA and SMS [19].

2.2.5. Development of an Offline-Capable Barangay Health Management System (Local)

In 2024, Lim and Torres created a mobile PWA for barangay health records with offline synchronization using Firebase, which reduced 50% of data entry time across two pilot sites. However, it was designed for health data tracking alone and excluded general document requests or printing [20]. This validates PWA-BaaS hybrids for barangays; the Smart Barangay Kiosk extends this to multi-document workflows with hardware integration.

2.2.6. Smart Kiosk for Local Government Services Using Raspberry Pi (Foreign)

Chen et al. (2023) set up kiosks using Raspberry Pi technology in townships across China for permit applications, incorporating touch interfaces with thermal printers for approximately 95% uptime in low-power environments [21]. The offline mode managed around 80% of transactions; however, it required culture-related adjustments for other contexts. This study localizes the concept using a tablet kiosk with Supabase and PhilSMS for the Philippines to adapt to DILG protocols.

2.2.7. E-Barangay Portal with SMS Integration for Resident Services (Local)

Dela Cruz et al. (2023) developed an online portal for clearances with Twilio SMS notifications, resulting in 40% better response rates in urban barangays. Yet, this resulted in a 25% failure rate during offline use because of outages. This upholds the SMS utility, which the kiosk-combined full offline PWA offers to ensure coverage [22].

2.2.8. Enhanced Barangay Information Management System with Residency Certificate Issuance (Local)

Imus and colleagues (2024) designed a web-based system for the issuance of certificates of residency, evaluated via USE questionnaire among officials and IT practitioners (mean score: 4.58/5) [23]. It had the requisite infrastructure for web-based issuance but no offline kiosk hardware or multi-document support. This study extends it by incorporating PWA offline synchronization and thermal auto-printing for walk-ins.

2.2.9. One Barangay: A Mobile and Web Barangay Management System (Local)

According to Mirasol et al. (2023), they created a hybrid mobile-web system to centralize record keeping and avoid corrupt data caused by manual Excel usage. Testing in urban settings showed improved security, but one limitation was the need for persistent connectivity, excluding offline conditions prevalent in rural settings [24]. The Smart Barangay Kiosk addresses this by providing Supabase local cache and IndexedDB durability for rural areas.

2.2.10. Barangay Certificate Issuance and Record Management System with Descriptive Analytics (Local)

Navarro et al. (2025) developed a web application using PHP/MySQL for the analytics of blotter and population reports that enable sorting data by gender/age. While this provided better understanding for administrators, it lacked self-service kiosks or SMS [25]. This informs the dashboard analytics extended here with offline PWA requests and real-time notifications.

2.2.11. A Risk Assessment of a Barangay Management System Portal (Local)

Studies by Fernando et al. (2024) have shown that among web portals geared towards developmental programs, ICT deficiencies and risks from manual operation were within Philippine barangays [26]. Data handling vulnerabilities were mentioned, but hardware solutions were not recommended. The tablet-based kiosk addresses this with encrypted offline storage, role-based access, and audit logging [26].

2.3. Synthesis and Research Gap

When synthesizing all these sources, a trend in Philippine e-Governance becomes evident: a progression from simple digitization (Hernandez & Cruz, 2021; Reyes & Salonga, 2021), to partial offline solutions (Lim & Torres, 2024; Santos et al., 2025), inspired partly by international kiosks (Adekunle & Oladipo, 2019; Chen et al., 2023). Newer entries such as Imus et al. (2024) and Navarro et al. (2025) leaned more on usability and analytics. Nevertheless, gaps remain: no integrated system provides 100% offline self-service via PWAs and physical kiosks, along with auto-printing through thermal printers, SMS notifications, and multi-document support—all deployable in true multi-barangay scenarios.

This study fills these gaps by deploying a Supabase-powered PWA on a tablet kiosk with Bluetooth thermal printer in two barangays, supporting cash-only pickups and admin management. These findings are relevant to national e-Government goals, potentially indicating scalable tools for rural adoption and addressing connectivity challenges through auto-sync.

The process includes planning, system design, prototyping, testing, and evaluation. 3.2. Requirement Analysis and Data Collection Methods

To guarantee that the Smart Barangay Kiosk System provides actual services and addresses the real needs and challenges of barangay service delivery, multiple data collection techniques were employed. These were selected to be the most relevant qualitative and quantitative data to inform the design, development, and functionality of the system.

3.2.1. Interviews and Surveys

Structured interviews and guided surveys were conducted with the selected barangay officials and residents of Barangay Maangas and Barangay Sta. Maria in Presentacion, Camarines Sur. The objective was to understand the current mechanisms of service delivery from both the administrative and end-user perspectives. Barangay officials, such as the secretary, clerks, and the barangay captain, provided insights into common challenges they face, including document processing bottlenecks, data storage issues, and communication barriers with residents, as well as needs for offline operation and SMS notifications.

Similarly, residents from diverse backgrounds—including senior citizens, persons with disabilities (PWDs), and low-income individuals—were surveyed to determine their level of access to digital tools, common service needs, and barriers encountered when availing of barangay services. The survey included both closed and open-ended questions to capture quantitative data (frequency of service use, waiting times) and qualitative feedback (satisfaction levels, suggestions for improvements).

Four structured survey instruments were developed and deployed through the system’s built-in survey module, each using a 5-point Likert scale (1 = Strongly Disagree, 2 = Disagree, 3 = Neutral, 4 = Agree, 5 = Strongly Agree). The instruments and their items are as follows:

A. Resident Pre-Usage Survey (15 items)

Administered before the resident uses the kiosk system, covering four parts:

  • Part 1 – Current Experience with Document Requests (5 items):
    1. I am satisfied with the current manual process of requesting barangay documents.
    2. The current process of requesting barangay documents is fast and efficient.
    3. I find it convenient to personally go to the barangay hall every time I need a document.
    4. I rarely experience long waiting times when requesting documents at the barangay.
    5. I am always informed about the status of my document request (e.g., if it is ready for pickup).
  • Part 2 – Accessibility and Inclusivity (3 items):
    1. The barangay’s current document request process is accessible to all residents, including senior citizens and PWDs.
    2. I can easily request barangay documents even during my work hours or busy schedule.
    3. I have no difficulty communicating my document request needs to barangay staff.
  • Part 3 – Technology Readiness (4 items):
    1. I am comfortable using digital devices such as smartphones, tablets, or computers.
    2. I am familiar with using self-service kiosks or touch-screen machines (e.g., ATMs, payment terminals).
    3. I have access to a smartphone or computer with internet connection at home.
    4. I am open to using a computerized system for requesting barangay documents.
  • Part 4 – Perceived Need for Digital Innovation (3 items):
    1. I believe the barangay needs a digital/computerized system for processing document requests.
    2. A system that allows me to request documents from home (online) would be very helpful.
    3. Receiving SMS notifications about my document request status would improve my experience.

B. Resident Post-Usage Survey (23 items)

Administered after the resident has used the kiosk system, covering six parts:

  • Part 1 – Ease of Use / Usability (5 items):
    1. The system was easy to understand and navigate, even without prior training.
    2. The on-screen instructions and labels were clear and easy to follow.
    3. I was able to complete my document request without needing assistance from barangay staff.
    4. The system’s interface design is user-friendly and visually appealing.
    5. I feel confident that I can use the system again on my own in the future.
  • Part 2 – Efficiency and Speed (3 items):
    1. Using the system, I was able to request a document faster compared to the traditional manual process.
    2. The number of steps required to complete a request was reasonable and not excessive.
    3. The system processed my request without significant delays or errors.
  • Part 3 – Accessibility and Convenience (4 items):
    1. The kiosk at the barangay hall is easy to access and use for walk-in residents.
    2. The option to request documents online (remotely) is very convenient.
    3. The system makes barangay document requests more accessible to senior citizens, PWDs, and busy residents.
    4. I can request documents at a time that is convenient for me, not limited to office hours.
  • Part 4 – Reliability and Offline Capability (3 items):
    1. The system worked properly even when internet connection was slow or unavailable.
    2. I did not experience any system crash or major technical issue during my use.
    3. I trust that my document request was properly recorded and will be processed.
  • Part 5 – SMS Notification and Communication (3 items):
    1. I received an SMS notification about the status of my document request.
    2. The SMS notification was timely and helped me know when to pick up my document.
    3. The SMS feature reduced the need for me to follow up in person at the barangay hall.
  • Part 6 – Overall Satisfaction and Impact (5 items):
    1. Overall, I am satisfied with my experience using the Smart Barangay Kiosk System.
    2. The system significantly improved the way I request barangay documents compared to the old process.

3.2.2. Observation

Direct, non-intrusive observations were conducted in Barangay Maangas and Barangay Sta. Maria in Presentacion, Camarines Sur to assess the existing service delivery workflow. The research team visited barangay halls during regular office hours to observe how documents are requested and processed, how residents interact with staff, how announcements are disseminated, and how records are maintained and stored.

The following key observations were noted during the site visits:

  1. Manual, paper-based workflow: Both barangay halls relied entirely on handwritten forms and logbooks for recording document requests. Residents were required to fill out a physical request form, after which the secretary or acting secretary manually typed the document using a word processor or pre-formatted template. This process was time-consuming and prone to typographical errors.
  2. No centralized record-keeping: Document request logs were maintained in physical ledgers or, in some cases, basic spreadsheet files on a single computer. There was no centralized digital database, making it difficult to retrieve historical records, track pending requests, or generate summary reports.
  3. In-person follow-ups: Residents had no way to check the status of their document request remotely. It was common for residents to return to the barangay hall to follow up, sometimes multiple times, contributing to congestion during peak hours. This was corroborated by the admin pre-usage survey, where “Residents frequently follow up in person” received a mean rating of 4.22.
  4. Limited office hours and accessibility: Barangay services were available only during regular office hours, creating difficulty for working residents. Senior citizens and persons with disabilities (PWDs) also had to personally visit the barangay hall, as no remote or self-service option was available. The resident pre-usage survey confirmed this, with “I can easily request documents during work hours” receiving the lowest accessibility rating (M = 2.33).
  5. Single point of failure: In both barangays, document processing depended primarily on the barangay secretary or a designated acting secretary. When this individual was absent or busy, document processing stalled. This observation reinforced the need for a system that can accept requests independently of staff availability.
  6. Announcement dissemination: Barangay announcements were primarily posted on physical bulletin boards or shared through Facebook groups. There was no integrated system for pushing announcements directly to residents at the point of service.
  7. Intermittent internet connectivity: Both barangay halls experienced frequent internet disruptions, confirming the necessity of an offline-first design that can continue accepting and queuing document requests without connectivity.

These observations directly informed the system’s design decisions, particularly the self-service kiosk interface (addressing items 1 and 5), the centralized Supabase database (item 2), SMS notifications (item 3), the remote resident portal (item 4), the events/announcements module (item 6), and the offline-first architecture using IndexedDB and Workbox service workers (item 7).

3.2.3. Document Analysis

To ensure the kiosk system’s compatibility with actual administrative barangay procedures, a thorough review of existing documents and forms was conducted, including certificates commonly issued (Certificate of Indigency, Barangay Clearance, Certificate of Residency, Certificate of Good Moral Character, Certification for First Time Job Seeker, Certification for Monthly Income, Certification for Solo Parent, Certification PDA, Business Clearance, Barangay ID, Barangay Certification), feedback and complaint forms, logs of previous document requests and response times, announcement formats, and filing systems (manual records, Excel sheets, logbooks).

The document analysis was complemented by structured survey data gathered from a total of 9 barangay officials and staff—specifically the barangay secretaries and acting secretaries who are directly responsible for document processing. Five (5) respondents came from Barangay Maangas and four (4) from Barangay Sta. Maria, Presentacion, Camarines Sur. Each respondent completed both a pre-usage survey (14 items) and a post-usage survey (19 items) through the system’s built-in survey module.

The following findings emerged from the document analysis and the administrative survey data:

  1. Document templates and formatting: Physical document templates were collected from both barangays. The formats varied slightly between the two (e.g., header layouts, signatory positions, official seal placement), confirming the need for a configurable, per-barangay template system rather than a one-size-fits-all approach. The system’s PDF generation module was designed with this flexibility, supporting customizable barangay name, officials, signatures, seal images, and pricing per document type.
  2. Volume and types of requests: Both barangays reported that Barangay Certification, Certificate of Indigency, and Barangay Clearance are the three most frequently requested documents. This was validated during the pilot deployment, where these three types accounted for 76.7% of the 86 documents released (32 Barangay Certifications, 20 Certificates of Indigency, and 14 Barangay Clearances).
  3. Record-keeping challenges: Admin pre-usage survey data confirmed that record-keeping and report generation is the most time-consuming aspect of the current workflow, scoring the highest challenge rating (M = 4.78, SD = 0.44). Physical logbooks showed inconsistent entries, missing dates, and difficulty locating past records, further underscoring the need for a centralized digital database with searchable release logs.
  4. Verification difficulties: Secretaries reported difficulty verifying resident information for document processing (M = 4.56, SD = 0.73 on the admin pre-usage survey). The existing process relied on the secretary’s personal knowledge of residents or on manually checking registration logbooks. This informed the system’s resident database and verification module, which provides a searchable resident registry with personal details, zone information, and account status.
  5. Workflow inefficiency during peak hours: The admin pre-usage survey showed strong agreement (M = 4.44, SD = 0.53) that managing requests during peak hours is difficult under the current manual process. The system addressed this through a digital request queue with status tracking (pending → in progress → ready for pickup → released), enabling concurrent request acceptance via the kiosk while the secretary processes earlier requests.
  6. Post-deployment improvements: After using the system, the same 9 admin respondents rated the system with a grand mean of 4.52 out of 5.00 across all 19 post-usage items. Notably, record-keeping and release log viewing scored M = 4.67 (post) versus M = 4.78 for the pre-usage challenge of manual record-keeping, confirming that the system directly addressed the identified bottleneck. Overall satisfaction among admin respondents reached M = 4.72, with all 9 respondents indicating they would recommend the system to other barangays (M = 4.78).
3.3. System Design, Development, and Implementation

The Smart Barangay Kiosk System integrates affordable hardware and flexible open-source software technologies for cost-effectiveness, scalability, and sustainability. The tools and technologies chosen match the objectives of the project, particularly the need for offline capabilities, easy deployment, and user access in resource-constrained barangay settings.

The kiosk setup consists of the following hardware:

The tablet is stationed at the barangay hall for walk-in residents to interact with the system.
  2. Bluetooth Thermal Printer (GOOJPRT PT-210): A 58mm compact, low-maintenance Bluetooth thermal printer is used to auto-print receipts for walk-in document requests. 3.3.2. Software and Development Tools
  1. Frontend Technologies (React 19 + Vite): The user interface is developed as a Progressive Web App (PWA) using React 19 for component-based architecture and Vite for fast development and production bundling. TailwindCSS provides utility-first styling. This enables a single app that works seamlessly on kiosk tablets, phones, and desktop browsers, with offline support via Workbox service workers.
  2. Backend and Database (Supabase): Supabase handles backend services, authentication, real-time database management, storage, and edge functions. It provides PostgreSQL as the database, row-level security (RLS) for multi-tenant data isolation, and automatic syncing upon internet reconnection. The free tier (500,000 rows) is sufficient for barangay-level needs.
  3. Offline Sync (IndexedDB + Workbox): The custom offline storage module uses IndexedDB (database name: sbk-offline) with four object stores: barangays, residents, settings, and pendingRequests. The settings store doubles as a general-purpose cache for resident portal data (session credentials, profile, resident record, document options, pricing, announcements, zone counts, and request history) using prefixed keys. Workbox runtime caching uses NetworkFirst strategy for Supabase REST API calls (5-second timeout, 200-entry limit, 24-hour expiry) and CacheFirst strategy for Supabase Storage assets. When connectivity is restored, the useOfflineSync hook automatically flushes queued requests from the pendingRequests store to Supabase.
  4. Thermal Printing (RawBT + ESC/POS): Receipt printing is achieved through the RawBT Android app, which bridges the PWA to the Bluetooth thermal printer via Android intent URL scheme (rawbt:{encoded_text}). Receipt content is formatted for 32 characters per line (58mm paper width) and includes barangay name, date, reference number, queue number, resident name, document list, pricing, and a footer message.
  5. SMS Notification (PhilSMS): Integration with PhilSMS provides affordable SMS notifications in the Philippines when documents are ready for pickup. The system also supports Semaphore as an alternative provider. SMS edge functions handle phone number normalization (PH 63-format), OTP generation for password recovery (6-digit codes, 5-minute expiry), and document-ready notifications.
  6. Hosting: The PWA is hosted on Vercel for the frontend. Supabase free tier handles the backend, database, and edge functions. Dynamic manifest switching allows separate PWA installs for kiosk mode (/kiosk), admin mode (/admin), and super-admin mode (/superadmin).
  7. PDF Generation (jsPDF): Document PDFs are generated client-side using the jsPDF library. Each document type has a dedicated generator supporting mixed bold/italic text formatting, official signatures and seal images, date formatting, barangay official details, pricing display, and word wrapping.
  8. Security Measures:
    • Authentication: Supabase Auth with email/password for admins and residents; separate bcrypt-hashed kiosk password per barangay stored in app_settings.
    • Access Control: Role-based access (superadmin, barangay_admin, resident) enforced via RLS policies. The current_admin_barangay() and is_superadmin() PostgreSQL functions enforce tenant isolation.
    • Rate Limiting: Kiosk password validation capped at 5 attempts per 15 minutes.
    • OTP Password Recovery: SMS-based OTP codes with 5-minute expiry and attempt tracking.
    • Audit Logging: All superadmin and admin actions are logged in the audit_logs table with actor, action, target, and metadata.
    • Session Management: Automatic idle reset after 2 minutes on the kiosk interface; lazy Supabase client creation per route to prevent token refresh issues.
3.3.3. System Architecture and Module Design

The system is architected as a multi-tenant PWA with four primary interfaces:

  1. Kiosk Interface (/kiosk): The public-facing self-service terminal deployed on the tablet. Residents search their name, select document types, fill in required fields (purpose, CTC number for business clearances), and submit. The system generates a control number, prints a thermal receipt, and queues the request for admin processing. If the system is offline, the request is saved to IndexedDB and synced when connectivity returns.
  2. Resident Portal (/resident): A login-protected interface for verified residents to submit document requests remotely from their phones, view request history with status tracking, submit feedback and ratings on released documents, and manage their profile information. After a resident’s account is verified at least once while online, the portal caches session credentials, profile data, barangay information, document options, pricing, announcements, and request history in IndexedDB. Subsequent logins can proceed entirely offline using the cached session, and document requests submitted offline are queued locally with an offline reference number and automatically uploaded when internet connectivity returns. An offline status banner informs the user of connectivity state and pending request count.
  3. Admin Dashboard (/admin): A comprehensive management panel with nine tabs:
    • Dashboard: Daily summary with key metrics (new submissions, pending verifications, announcements, pricing gaps), officials status, and calendar events.
    • Manage Residents: CRUD operations for residents with bulk import, search, and zone assignment.
    • Requests: Document request workflow (Pending → In Progress → Ready for Pickup) with SMS alerts, payment logging, release confirmation, and chat panel for internal notes.
    • Verification: Review and approve/reject resident registration submissions.
    • Calendar: Admin-created events with holiday integration.
    • Announcements: Create/edit barangay announcements with image uploads and date ranges.
    • Pricing: Configure document service fees per barangay.
    • Feedback: View resident ratings (1–5 stars) and comments with CSV export.
    • Barangay Info: Manage officials, zone settings, feature toggles, and seal upload.
  4. Super-Admin Panel (/superadmin): A system-wide management interface with thirteen tabs for overview, analytics, broadcast announcements, tenant management (create/edit/delete barangays), admin user management, system-wide resident and request views, document configuration, audit logs, feedback aggregation, data cleanup utilities, access and security settings, and thesis document generation.
3.3.4. System Flowchart

Figure 3.3 illustrates the complete system flowchart of the Smart Barangay Kiosk System, showing the four user entry points and their respective workflows converging on the shared cloud backend.

The system operates through four distinct interfaces, each serving a specific user role:

  1. Walk-in Kiosk Flow (Tablet at Barangay Hall): The resident is greeted by a welcome screen displaying barangay announcements. The kiosk checks whether the resident exists in the database via name search (querying the IndexedDB cache first, then Supabase if online). If found, the resident’s profile is auto-populated; if not, a new applicant intake form collects demographics. The resident then selects the desired document(s), adds purpose and CTC details if applicable, and reviews the calculated pricing (document price + service fee). Upon submission, the system checks connectivity: if online, the request is POSTed directly to Supabase; if offline, it is queued in the IndexedDB pendingRequests store for automatic synchronization when connectivity returns. A thermal receipt is printed via the RawBT Android app to the GOOJPRT PT-210 Bluetooth printer. Finally, the resident is prompted for feedback (1–5 stars + optional comment) and a Likert-scale survey. The kiosk auto-resets after 2 minutes of idle time.
  2. Resident Portal Flow (Remote Web Access): Remote residents authenticate via email and password through Supabase Auth. New users register and link their account to an existing resident record (by matching name, birthday, zone, and contact details) or register as a new resident. Once linked, residents can select documents, submit requests (source: resident_portal), view request history and release logs, provide feedback on released documents, and complete pre/post-usage surveys. The portal supports offline session caching via IndexedDB for continued access during connectivity interruptions.
  3. Admin Dashboard Flow (Secretary/Staff): Barangay officials log in with email and password (verified against the admin_users table). The dashboard presents a request pipeline with four statuses: Pending (new submissions from kiosk and portal) → Current (being processed by staff) → Done (ready for pickup; triggers PDF generation via jsPDF and SMS notification via PhilSMS) → Released (archived with payment recorded in release logs). Additional management tabs enable resident verification (approve/reject new profiles), resident database management, announcements scheduling, document pricing configuration, barangay information and officials setup, and feedback review. Admin pre- and post-usage surveys are triggered at login and after the first document release, respectively.
  4. Super Admin Flow (System-wide Management): The super admin accesses a centralized panel for multi-tenant oversight, including a health dashboard per barangay, cross-barangay analytics with CSV export, broadcast announcements, tenant creation and configuration, admin user management, audit logs, and system-wide settings.

Offline-First Synchronization: When a kiosk request is submitted without internet connectivity, it is stored in the IndexedDB pendingRequests object store. The useOfflineSync React hook monitors network status and, upon detecting connectivity, automatically flushes all queued requests to Supabase. Successfully synced requests are removed from the local queue; failed requests remain for the next sync attempt. Workbox service workers provide runtime caching for Supabase REST API responses (NetworkFirst strategy, 5-second timeout, 200-entry limit, 24-hour expiry) and Supabase Storage assets (CacheFirst strategy).

Cloud Backend: All four interfaces converge on the Supabase cloud backend, which comprises a PostgreSQL database with Row-Level Security (RLS) policies enforcing multi-tenant data isolation, Supabase Auth for JWT-based authentication across all user roles, and Edge Functions for SMS dispatch (PhilSMS), OTP generation, kiosk password validation, and administrative user management.

3.7. Development Boundaries and Prototype Constraints
  • Prototype-Stage Deployment: The system was developed as a functional prototype for pilot testing only; full-scale rollout is not within the current study’s objective.
  • Standalone Operation: The kiosk operates as an independent device. Development does not include integration to national government platforms or third-party mobile applications beyond Supabase and PhilSMS. No online payment or QR codes are implemented; payment is cash only upon pickup.
  • Security Implementation: The prototype implements role-based access control, bcrypt password hashing, rate limiting, OTP recovery, and audit logging. Advanced penetration testing and vulnerability assessment are not within scope.
  • Short-Term Evaluation Focus: Evaluation is limited to measuring the pilot system’s functionality, ease of use, and administrative utility. Long-term impact and sustainability assessment are not covered.
3.8. Ethical Considerations

This research maintains strict compliance with ethical standards for research to guarantee the rights, privacy, and welfare of all participants. The following procedures were instituted:

  • Informed Consent: An informed consent document, clear and comprehensible, was provided to all respondents before any survey, interview, usability test, or observation session. Data collection included only individuals who provided written or verbal consent.
  • Confidentiality and Anonymity: Participants’ confidentiality was strictly upheld. Responses were anonymized, and identifying details are not published in any reports.
  • Data Privacy and Protection: In compliance with the Data Privacy Act of 2012 (Republic Act No. 10173), all collected data is stored in secure, password-protected systems. Only authorized members of the research team have access to raw data [27].
  • Ethical System Design: The system includes role-based access control, automatic session timeout (2-minute idle reset on kiosk), bcrypt password hashing, and restricted administrative access to protect sensitive resident data. The system was built on privacy-by-design principles.
  • Vulnerable Populations: Special consideration was given to vulnerable groups such as senior citizens, persons with disabilities (PWDs), and low-income residents. Research activities were conducted in a respectful, inclusive, and non-discriminatory manner.
  • Ethical Review: The research protocol was submitted to the appropriate institutional body for ethical clearance and approval prior to field activities [28].
Chapter 4
CONCLUSION

This study developed and evaluated the Smart Barangay Kiosk System with Offline-First Document Request Handling and Resident Management, deployed across two pilot barangays—Maangas and Sta. Maria, Presentacion, Camarines Sur. The system was tested with 46 resident respondents and 9 barangay officials and staff, yielding 86 document releases across 9 document types and 74 feedback entries during the pilot period. The conclusions addressing each specific objective are as follows:

Objective 1: Develop a kiosk interface for document requests. The walk-in kiosk interface was successfully developed and deployed in both pilot barangays. Residents rated the system’s ease of use at M = 4.19 and interface design at M = 4.28 on a 5-point Likert scale, confirming that the touch-screen interface is intuitive and user-friendly even without prior training. Of the 46 resident respondents, 69.6% used the kiosk as walk-in users, validating its role as the primary point of interaction.

Objective 2: Construct a secure centralized database. The Supabase-powered centralized database was implemented with multi-tenant architecture supporting both pilot barangays under a single deployment. A total of 97 residents were successfully registered in the system (64 in Maangas, 33 in Sta. Maria), and all 86 document releases were properly recorded with complete audit trails. Admin respondents rated the system’s data reliability at M = 4.48, with no reports of data loss (M = 4.67 on that specific item).

Objective 3: Create a web-based admin dashboard. The admin dashboard was developed and used by 9 barangay officials for processing document requests, managing residents, and releasing documents. The dashboard received high usability ratings (M = 4.47 for ease of use, M = 4.78 for layout and design), and admin respondents confirmed that it improved their workflow management, particularly for request tracking (M = 4.67) and record-keeping (M = 4.67).

Objective 4: Automate receipt printing and SMS notifications. Bluetooth thermal receipt printing was integrated with the kiosk for issuing transaction receipts upon document request submission. SMS notifications were implemented for informing residents when their documents are ready for pickup. Admin respondents rated the SMS feature’s usefulness at M = 4.22. Resident SMS evaluation scores were low (M = 2.41) due to evaluation timing—walk-in users completed the survey before documents were processed and SMS was triggered—rather than a functional deficiency.

Objective 5: Develop offline-first functionality. The offline-first architecture using service workers and IndexedDB was successfully implemented, enabling the kiosk to accept document requests even during internet outages. Resident respondents rated reliability at M = 4.33, and the specific item on system performance during slow or unavailable connectivity scored M = 4.41. Admin respondents similarly validated offline reliability (M = 4.22), confirming the system’s suitability for deployment in rural areas with intermittent internet connectivity.

Objective 6: Implement authentication and security. Role-based access control was implemented with three distinct user roles (Super Admin, Admin, Kiosk), complemented by bcrypt password hashing, automatic 2-minute idle session timeout on the kiosk, OTP-based resident verification via SMS, and Row-Level Security (RLS) policies in the database. Admin respondents rated the resident verification feature at M = 4.44, confirming its effectiveness in ensuring only legitimate residents can request documents.

Objective 7: Test and evaluate usability, reliability, and efficiency in pilot barangays. Comprehensive pre- and post-usage surveys were conducted with both resident and admin respondent groups. The resident grand mean increased from M = 3.38 (pre-usage) to M = 4.11 (post-usage), and the admin grand mean increased from M = 4.09 to M = 4.52, indicating significant improvement in perceived service quality. The overall user satisfaction was M = 4.67 for residents and M = 4.72 for admin respondents. The feedback module recorded an overall mean rating of 4.55 out of 5.00, with 73% of respondents giving 5 stars. These results confirm that the system meets its design objectives of usability, reliability, and efficiency.

Overall, the Smart Barangay Kiosk System demonstrates that a tablet-based, offline-first digital kiosk with a Bluetooth thermal printer is a viable and effective approach for modernizing document request processing at the barangay level. The dual-channel design (walk-in kiosk and remote web portal) successfully broadened access to residents with varying schedules and mobility constraints. The multi-tenant architecture proved scalable across two pilot sites, and the system’s consistent high ratings from both residents and administrators indicate strong potential for wider adoption in Philippine local government units seeking to advance e-governance and digital inclusion.

RECOMMENDATION

Based on the findings and conclusions of this study, the following recommendations are proposed for future research and system enhancement:

  1. Expansion to Additional Barangays and Municipalities. The system should be deployed in additional barangays beyond Presentacion, Camarines Sur to validate scalability and adaptability across diverse local government contexts. A phased rollout to neighboring municipalities would provide data on multi-site administration and resource sharing.
  2. Integration with National Government Digital Infrastructure. Future iterations should explore integration with the Philippine Identification System (PhilSys) and other national government databases to enable automated identity verification, reducing the need for manual resident registration and improving data accuracy.
  3. Biometric Authentication for Resident Verification. Adding fingerprint or facial recognition as an authentication option on the kiosk would strengthen security and simplify the verification process, particularly for residents who may have difficulty with OTP-based SMS verification.
  4. Native Mobile Application for Residents. While the current system provides a responsive web portal for remote document requests, a dedicated native mobile application (Android/iOS) could offer improved performance, push notification support, and offline access for residents using smartphones.
  5. Enhanced SMS Notification Coverage. Given the low SMS evaluation scores among kiosk walk-in users (M = 2.41), future studies should ensure that the evaluation timeline allows respondents to experience the full document lifecycle, including SMS notification upon release. Additionally, alternative notification channels (e.g., push notifications, messaging apps) may be explored to supplement SMS.
  6. Analytics and Reporting Dashboard. Implementing advanced data analytics and visualization tools within the admin dashboard would enable barangay officials to generate insights on document request trends, peak demand periods, and resident demographics for data-driven decision-making.
  7. Long-Term Sustainability and Cost-Effectiveness Study. A longitudinal study measuring operational costs, maintenance requirements, and sustained user satisfaction over 12–24 months of continuous deployment would provide critical data for policy recommendations on digital kiosk adoption in LGUs.
  8. Multi-Language Support. Adding Filipino and Bicol language options for the kiosk interface and resident-facing content would enhance accessibility and inclusivity, particularly for elderly residents and those less comfortable with English.
  9. Integration with Online Payment Systems. Future versions should support cashless payment processing through integration with GCash, Maya, or other local digital payment platforms, enabling end-to-end digital transactions without requiring cash handling at the barangay hall.
  10. Comprehensive Cybersecurity Assessment. A formal penetration testing and security audit should be conducted to identify and address potential vulnerabilities, particularly as the system handles sensitive personal data governed by the Data Privacy Act of 2012 (RA 10173).
